How Avalon makes money
Primary model
Direct-to-consumer mobile IV — book online via Acuity Scheduling, nurse comes to you. No membership model visible. Cash / card per session only.
Events channel
On-site IV for weddings, corporate, festivals, film sets, wellness retreats. Quote-based, not publicly priced. Higher-ticket, higher-margin per session than individual bookings.
Partnerships channel
Revenue share or referral arrangement with other clinicians (MDs, NPs, PAs, RNs). Likely a referral fee or split per client sent. No public rates — contact-form gated.
Pricing strategy
Entry at $150 (Dehydration). Standard treatments: $250 flat across most IVs. Premium tier: NAD+ $350–$1,200, CBD $250–$400, Exosomes $700–$1,800. No membership or subscriptions anywhere on the site.
Booking
Acuity Scheduling (as.me links). Standard platform, not custom-built. Single CTA across the entire site: "Start Your Recovery."
Clinical leadership
MD-founded (Dr. Jayson Weir — internal medicine + regenerative therapies). RN clinical lead (Stephanie Weeks). Team of ~6 shown. Positions as medical-grade, not spa-grade.
Brand positioning
"Mobile recovery therapy" — targets SF tech workers, festival-goers, athletes, artists. Language: "elite," "engineered," "high performance." Active new-client promo (15% off code).
Geographic reach
Bay Area primary. "California and beyond" claimed. Appeared at Hereticon (SF tech conference). ZIP checker implies demand-based geographic expansion model.
Hours + contact
Mon–Sun 8AM–8PM. Phone: (415) 980-7708. Email: support@avalonvitality.co.
Social
X (Twitter), Facebook, Instagram. No TikTok visible. No follower counts shown on site.

What's missing from Avalon's menu
No membership
No recurring subscription model anywhere on the site. Every client is a one-time transaction. This is their biggest structural revenue weakness.
No GLP-1
Weight management and metabolic programs entirely absent. Fast-growing, high-margin, high-LTV category.
No hormone services
No HRT, perimenopause care, or concierge NP services. Purely IV-focused — clinical scope stops there.
No concierge / VIP tier
No named premium ongoing client tier. Events are the closest analog but aren't positioned as a recurring high-touch relationship.

Where NCTS wins
Clear win
Membership model — Avalon has none
Avalon is a pure transactional model. Every client is a one-time booking. NCTS's $299/month membership creates predictable MRR, higher LTV, and a structural reason clients stay. This is the single biggest advantage you have over them — and it doesn't require more clients, just more committed ones.
Clear win
GLP-1 + hormone services — Avalon is IV-only
Avalon's menu stops at IV therapy. NCTS's Phase 2 roadmap (GLP-1, NAD, perimenopause/HRT) enters a completely adjacent, high-margin, recurring market Avalon isn't touching. Kimberly's NP scope makes this possible — Avalon would need to restructure their entire clinical model to compete here.
Clear win
Relationship brand vs. performance brand — different client entirely
Avalon's language targets tech bros, festival performers, athletes. NCTS's "NP best friend" positioning hits women in wellness, perimenopause, professional self-care. These are different clients with different LTV profiles and almost no overlap — NCTS isn't directly competing with Avalon, they're opening a different lane.
Clear win
Kelly's partnership background is a structural advantage Avalon can't replicate
Avalon has a contact form. Kelly has 15 years of building partner programs with revenue share, tracking, and structured activation. NCTS can build a real partner channel — QR codes, dedicated booking pages, monthly reporting — that turns pop-up locations into a distribution network. Avalon isn't doing this systematically.
Watch closely
Avalon has a head start in the same geography
They're already operating in Oakland/SF, already at Hereticon, already hiring. They will show up in the same ZIP codes and on the same Google searches. NCTS needs a clear, memorable differentiation reason visible from the first touchpoint — the membership, the relationship positioning, and Kimberly's clinical credibility have to be the first things a prospective client sees.
Watch closely
Their $150 entry price undercuts NCTS's $300 average
Avalon's $150 Dehydration IV is a price-anchoring strategy — it sets the expectation that IV therapy is a $150–$250 product. NCTS can't win on price and shouldn't try. The answer is to out-relationship them, not out-price them. The membership math ($299/month for ongoing care vs. $250 per visit) reframes the comparison entirely when positioned correctly.
